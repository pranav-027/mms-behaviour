# 📖 The Story of Two Big Scams — Told Simply

Let me tell you two real stories about how **human emotions and mental shortcuts** caused two of the biggest financial disasters ever. This is what **Behavioural Finance** is all about — it studies how people make money decisions based on *feelings*, not *logic*.

---

## 🇺🇸 STORY 1: The Enron Story (USA, 2001)

### The Beginning — A "Superstar" Company
Once upon a time, there was a company called **Enron**. It started as a simple gas pipeline company in 1985. But its leaders — **Kenneth Lay, Jeffrey Skilling, and Andrew Fastow** — wanted to make it the *coolest, smartest* company in America.

They transformed it into a fancy "energy trading" company. Magazines called it one of the **"Most Admired Companies."** Everyone loved Enron. Its stock price kept climbing — almost **$90 per share!**

### The Trick Behind the Magic
But here's the secret: Enron was **lying about its profits.**

- They used a trick called **"Mark-to-Market accounting"** — meaning they recorded *future expected profits* as if they already earned them today. (Imagine telling your parents you "earned" ₹10,000 because you *might* get a job next year!)
- They created **fake side companies (SPEs)** to hide their massive debts — around **$13–30 billion** hidden from the public.
- The CFO, Andrew Fastow, secretly pocketed **$45 million** for himself.

### The Collapse
In 2001, the truth came out. Stock crashed from $90 → $30 → $10 → **$0**. 
- 20,000 employees lost their jobs and life savings.
- Investors lost **$74 billion**.
- The auditor (Arthur Andersen) was caught destroying documents and shut down.

---

### 🧠 How Behavioural Finance Explains Enron

| Bias | What Happened in Simple Words |
|------|-------------------------------|
| **Overconfidence Bias** | Bosses thought, "We're too smart to get caught." |
| **Greed & Incentive Bias** | Executives got bonuses based on stock price → so they faked the numbers to get richer. |
| **Herd Mentality** | Investors bought stocks because "everyone else is buying it." |
| **Authority Bias** | People blindly trusted Kenneth Lay because he was the "boss." Nobody questioned him. |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Investors only listened to good news and ignored warnings (like whistleblower Sherron Watkins shouting "FRAUD!"). |
| **Anchoring Bias** | Even when stock fell to $10, employees thought, "It was $90 before, it'll come back!" — and lost everything. |
| **Halo Effect** | Because Enron looked successful in one area, people assumed it was great everywhere. |

## 🇮🇳 STORY 2: The Sahara India Story

### The Beginning — The "People's Company"
In India, there was a man named **Subrata Roy**, who built a giant business empire called **Sahara India Pariwar**. They were everywhere — cricket sponsorships, movies, hotels, real estate, and even on Team India's jerseys! 🏏

Sahara collected money from **3 crore (30 million) ordinary people** — mostly from villages and small towns in UP, Bihar, MP, Rajasthan, etc. — promising them high returns.

They used something called **OFCDs (Optional Fully Convertible Debentures)** — a complicated financial product that most villagers didn't understand.

### The Problem
- Sahara collected around **₹24,000 crore** without proper rules.
- They didn't follow SEBI (the market regulator) rules.
- The whole system had no transparency.

### The Fall
In 2012, the **Supreme Court of India** ordered Sahara to **return ₹24,000 crore + 15% interest** to investors. 
- Subrata Roy went to jail.
- Many investors are *still* waiting for their money today, because records were missing or incomplete.

---

### 🧠 How Behavioural Finance Explains Sahara

| Bias | What Happened in Simple Words |
|------|-------------------------------|
| **Trust in Charisma** | Subrata Roy was famous and confident — people thought, "He looks honest, must be safe!" |
| **Familiarity Bias** | "Sahara is in our village, my neighbour invested too — must be okay." |
| **Authority Bias** | Sahara sponsored cricket and Bollywood — so people assumed it must be a *legit* big company. |
| **Halo Effect** | Because Sahara looked good in cricket/films, people thought their finance business was also trustworthy. |
| **Herd Behaviour** | "Everyone in my village is investing, so I will too!" |
| **FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)** | "If I don't invest now, I'll miss the chance to get rich!" |
| **Confirmation Bias** | People only listened to success stories, ignoring red flags. |

---

## 🎯 The Big Lesson — What Both Stories Teach Us

Both Enron and Sahara prove the **core idea of Behavioural Finance**:

> 💡 **People are NOT always rational with money. Emotions like greed, fear, trust, and FOMO control our decisions more than logic.**

### Simple Takeaways:
1. **Don't trust blindly** — even big famous companies can be fake. (Authority Bias)
2. **Don't follow the crowd** — just because everyone is investing doesn't mean it's safe. (Herd Behaviour)
3. **Don't fall in love with a stock** — old high prices don't predict future. (Anchoring Bias)
4. **Greed kills judgment** — if returns look "too good to be true," they probably are.
5. **Always ask questions** — transparency matters more than charisma.

---

### 🧾 In One Line:
> **Enron and Sahara collapsed not just because of fraud — but because human emotions made smart people do stupid things with their money.**

That's the power of **Behavioural Finance** — it explains *why* these disasters happen again and again. ✨
